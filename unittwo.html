1.5. Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites.
Copyright law in U.K. protects original contents of work whether that be music, art,
video or sound, this is protected under the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988.
A co existing law, the Copyright Regulations 1992 extends protection to software and
other forms of online work. Copyright applies upon creation, as soon as your website
is posted it is protected, however it is highly recommended to register your website
because websites are subject to abuse which will lead to a civil cause of action.
Copyright law does not protect logos, you will need a trademark for logos. [1]
1.6. Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that
people with disabilities may face when using web pages and how we can overcome them.
Issues that disabled people may face is if they are color blind as the color palate
for your website may make it hard for them to navigate through the pages. Accessibility
may also apply not only to disabled people as it allows unhindered access through means
such as media televisions and mobile phones, adding text alternatives to image tags may
help visually-impaired users this is done with alt attributes, also the use of a screen
reader which can translate your website into text to speech or a braille display. [2]

1.7. Describe which are the appropriate file types for websites.
There are two types of image file types, raster and vector, raster is a pixel based image
normally saved as JPG, PNG, GIF, these file types are mostly used for photos, whereas
vectors are not pixel based they are built using proportional formulas which makes them
perfect for logos as they made need resizing and vectors will stop the image pixelating.
Vector files are normally saved as SVG files. The best video file to use for websites is
AVI or MP4 as they are coded to be smaller than over video codecs. [3]
